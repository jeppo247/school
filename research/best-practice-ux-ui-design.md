# Design principles for an at-home AI tutor for children

## What the evidence points to

For a Prep-to-Year-7 AI tutor built around mastery learning, the strongest design conclusion is not “make it look more like a game,” but “remove as much unnecessary friction as possible while making mastery, next steps, and correction extremely clear.” The research base that begins with entity["people","Benjamin Bloom","education researcher"]’s mastery-and-tutoring framing and extends through modern computer-based feedback research points in the same direction: learners do best when they can proceed after demonstrated competence, receive immediate and informative correction, and are not overloaded by decorative interface choices. Cognitive-load research also consistently warns against split attention, seductive details, and animation that adds processing costs without adding instructional value. citeturn35search9turn30view0turn31view0turn32search1

In practice, that means the visual system should be calm rather than hyper-stimulating, the interaction model should be predictable rather than surprising, and the tutor should expose the child’s current mastery state in a simple self-referenced way rather than forcing them into grade-like or peer-comparison metaphors. In other words: sparse screens, obvious controls, short feedback loops, visible progress by skill, and strong error recovery. citeturn33view3turn33view4turn39view0turn30view4

## Visual system

The evidence does **not** support a single magic font, a single “best” child palette, or a universal “dyslexia font” solution. Studies and reviews of typography and dyslexia-focused fonts do not show a robust universal improvement from specialised fonts alone, which means your safest path is legibility plus personalisation rather than branding your way into readability. A practical evidence-based starting point is to follow accessibility floors of at least 16px body text, then set your actual defaults larger for children and expose text-size controls; for early readers, I would treat 16px as a minimum safety floor, not a design target. citeturn22search28turn11view0turn11view1

For colour, treat contrast and signalling as more important than “kid-like brightness.” The entity["organization","World Wide Web Consortium","web standards body"] guidelines require at least 4.5:1 contrast for normal text and 3:1 for large text, and they explicitly warn against conveying meaning by colour alone. Focus indicators also need enough visual change and contrast to be clearly noticed. For a tutor, that implies restrained palettes, strong dark-on-light or light-on-dark text contrast, and redundant state signalling such as icon + label + colour, rather than relying on green/red alone for correctness or mastery. citeturn42search7turn42search15turn42search1turn42search8

Animation should be used as instruction, not decoration. Cognitive-load reviews note that related information should sit near each other, irrelevant detail should be stripped out, and animation often increases extraneous load when a static representation would do the job better. Accessibility guidance also requires that moving, blinking, scrolling, or auto-updating content that persists be pausable, and cognitive-accessibility guidance warns that shifting content increases overload and disorientation. For this product, motion should exist mainly to demonstrate an action, confirm a state change, or direct attention to the next step. It should not be there to make the app feel “alive.” citeturn32search1turn41search16turn41search7turn34view4

The deeper design implication is that a child-facing tutor should look more like a very good accessibility-first product than like a loud children’s entertainment app. Use whitespace, consistent card structures, obvious headings, and familiar controls. Keep illustrations subordinate to the task. If art, mascots, or motion compete with the mathematical sentence, spelling prompt, or reading passage, they are probably harming learning rather than helping it. citeturn33view1turn33view3turn32search1

## Interaction mechanics

Young children are meaningfully different from adults in motor precision and gesture understanding, so the interaction model should skew heavily toward large tap targets and low-precision actions. Accessibility standards set a floor of 44 by 44 CSS pixels for targets, and child-gesture studies show that younger children are less accurate than adults, that success improves with age, and that very young children struggle more with certain gestures such as drag-and-drop, rotate, and spread. On that basis, I would treat 44 by 44 as the absolute minimum and make primary targets noticeably larger for Prep to Year 2. citeturn11view2turn26view0

The safest default gesture set is tap first, drag only when it matches the concept being taught, and almost never hidden or custom gestures. Research with preschoolers found that the mechanism used to prompt a gesture matters a great deal: modelling by a person was the most effective teaching tool, while pure visual state changes were the least effective, and children under about three were largely unable to follow in-app prompts reliably. Separate work also shows that when the gesture is conceptually congruent with the task, dragging can support better performance than tapping. So the rule should be: use direct manipulation only when it is semantically meaningful, and teach new gestures explicitly. citeturn28view2turn28view3turn25search1turn25search22

This is especially important for onboarding and help. Research on prompting shows that children are more likely to interpret an animated hand than a static symbol, and that terms such as “slide” and “swipe” are easier than interface jargon such as “select” or “press and hold.” For a mastery tutor, the UX consequence is straightforward: if a child seems stuck, the system should first show or model the action, then let the child try it, rather than dumping abstract text instructions onto the screen. citeturn26view0turn28view3

Navigation should also be cognitively simple. The cognitive-accessibility guidance recommends logical sections, common words, clear headings, visible indications of hidden content, and explicit relationships between controls and the content they affect. It also advises against multiple or nested scrolling areas. For children, that translates into one main objective per screen, one main action per step, fixed positions for Back and Help, and as few disclosure layers as possible. citeturn33view0turn33view1turn33view4

## Mastery learning UX

A mastery-learning tutor should organise the interface around **skills**, not lessons completed, calendar time, or classroom sequence. Competency-based design literature explicitly frames progress as moving on only after current tasks are mastered and at the learner’s own pace. In the interface, that means the primary progress object should be a skill map or a small set of current competencies, not a “Week 6,” “Unit 3,” or “Class average” framing. The learner should always be able to answer three questions at a glance: what skill am I on, what does success look like, and what should I do next if I am not there yet? citeturn38search3turn35search9

Progress visibility matters, but it should usually be self-referenced. Open learner modelling research shows that making the learner model visible can increase self-awareness and provide metacognitive support, and systems such as Mastery Grids combine progress visualisation with navigation so that the learner continually sees where they are. That is highly relevant to a mastery tutor: progress should not be hidden inside the AI; it should be surfaced in a simple, inspectable form. However, the same literature on peer-facing models shows that comparison features can cut both ways, which is one reason I would prioritise “your progress against the skill graph” over “your position relative to other children.” citeturn39view0turn38search7

Feedback is where the mastery UX either works or fails. The most consistent evidence is that elaborated feedback outperforms bare correctness feedback, especially for higher-order learning; feedback should focus on the task, not the learner; it should be specific, timely, and given in manageable units; and for difficult new tasks, immediate feedback is usually the safer choice. For novices and struggling learners, more directive feedback is beneficial; as expertise grows, support can fade and become more facilitative. That makes a very strong case for a tutor loop of: answer, immediate outcome, short explanation, optional hint ladder, retry, then mastery update. citeturn31view0turn30view0turn30view1turn30view3turn30view4turn35search3

The important nuance is that “lots of feedback” is not the same as “good feedback.” Shute’s review warns against overwhelming learners, against praise that redirects attention to self rather than task, and against progressive hints that always collapse into giving away the answer. In a mastery interface, a hint stack should therefore be layered: first reduce uncertainty, then point to the relevant feature, then offer a more concrete strategy, and only finally show a worked step if the child has exhausted reasonable attempts. citeturn30view0turn30view1turn30view4

## AI tutor behaviour and trust

Because this is an AI tutor rather than a static adaptive product, conversational design matters. Research on children and voice assistants shows that children can understand technology as a social partner and are affected by that understanding. That does **not** mean you should lean into artificial friendship. It means you should be very careful with anthropomorphism, social dependence cues, and emotional framing. The tutor should state clearly that it is an AI tool, avoid implying real feelings or needs, and never pressure the child to keep talking, confess personal information, or treat the system as a companion rather than a learning tool. citeturn16view1

Language should be literal, concrete, and low-memory. Cognitive-accessibility guidance recommends familiar design patterns, clear labels, simple sentence structure, and literal language, while also warning against interfaces that rely on remembering hidden functions or previous steps. For an AI tutor, each turn should therefore be short, specific, and anchored to the current task. Avoid compound instructions such as “First do X, then compare Y, then if that fails remember rule Z.” Prefer “Let’s do one thing: underline the verb,” followed by the next step only after the child responds. citeturn33view3turn34view3turn41search2

Support for mistakes needs to be very strong. Guidance from the same accessibility literature recommends predictable Back or Undo functions, no loss of work when going back, and no unexpected movement or layout changes that cause accidental taps. In a child-facing mastery tutor, every learning state should be recoverable. If the child opens the wrong hint, exits a question, or navigates away, they should be able to return to the same place with their work intact. citeturn34view4

Child-facing data and safety design should also be built into the UX, not tacked on later. The entity["organization","Information Commissioner's Office","uk regulator"] Children’s Code says services likely to be accessed by children should not use nudge techniques to push them into giving unnecessary data or weakening privacy protections, and it expects prominent, accessible tools to help children exercise rights and report concerns. For your tutor, that strongly supports a visible “Ask an adult,” “Report this answer,” or “Something feels wrong” control on every tutor screen, along with privacy defaults that are conservative and not negotiated through manipulative UI. citeturn41search5turn41search1turn41search9

## Anti-patterns to avoid

The first anti-pattern is **performative stimulation**: floating rewards, pulsing backgrounds, ambient motion, autoplay transitions, decorative sound, and background activity that competes with the learning task. The literature on cognitive load and accessible motion is very clear that irrelevant detail, unexpected movement, and persistent motion create distraction and can increase mental fatigue. If motion does not teach the concept, confirm the result, or demonstrate the interaction, remove it. citeturn32search1turn41search16turn34view4

The second anti-pattern is **opaque navigation**: hidden menus, ambiguous icons, nested scroll regions, changing control positions, and controls separated from the content they influence. These patterns are especially harmful in a tutor because they add “how do I use this thing?” as an extra problem on top of the actual learning problem. Clear grouping, visible signposts, conventional controls, and a stable layout are not cosmetic niceties here; they are part of the pedagogy. citeturn33view0turn33view3turn33view4turn34view4

The third anti-pattern is **performance theatre masquerading as mastery**: grades-first feedback, praise-heavy chat, public rankings, streak loss, and peer comparison as the main progress model. The feedback literature shows that comments and task-focused information matter more than grade signals, and that praise can pull attention away from the task. Leaderboard evidence is mixed at best; some work finds possible motivational benefits, but other studies report no additional practice and even lower exam scores, while peer-model research finds competition can be both positive and negative. In a child mastery product, I would not make rank-order comparison a default mechanic. citeturn30view0turn30view1turn40search0turn40search8turn40search12turn38search7

The fourth anti-pattern is **magic-fix accessibility**: assuming one dyslexia font, one colour overlay, or one “kid mode” solves readability for everyone. The evidence base does not support a universal typeface cure. Personalisation is the safer design principle: adjustable text size, optional read-aloud, reduced motion, contrast themes, and persistent user preferences. citeturn11view0turn11view1turn33view2

The fifth anti-pattern is **time pressure**. WCAG guidance stresses that tasks should not rely on restrictive time limits, that disabling time limits is better than merely extending them, and that timeouts present barriers for people who need more time to read and act. A mastery tutor should therefore avoid countdowns for core learning tasks, avoid expiring work, and avoid forcing rapid responses unless timing is itself the skill being practised. citeturn41search0turn41search4turn41search8turn41search11

## Recommended baseline

If I were turning this research into a product specification for code generation, I would start with the following defaults.

- **Screen architecture:** one learning objective per screen, one primary action, one scroll region, stable Back/Help placement, and no nested scrolling. citeturn33view4turn33view0
- **Typography:** never below 16px for body text; start larger than that by default, especially for Prep to Year 2; allow persistent text-size adjustment; prefer a simple sans-serif with strong letter differentiation over novelty fonts. This larger default is my inference from the standards floor plus the lack of a universal special-font winner. citeturn22search28turn11view0turn11view1
- **Colour and state:** meet at least AA contrast, use colour plus text/icon labels, and give focus states a clear visible outline or border change. citeturn42search7turn42search15turn42search1turn42search8
- **Targets:** minimum 44×44 CSS px, with larger primary targets for younger children; use tap as the default interaction. Larger-than-minimum targets for early years are a design inference from child motor findings plus accessibility floors. citeturn11view2turn26view0
- **Motion:** only purposeful, short, and user-initiated; anything prolonged must be pausable; nothing important should move unless the learner caused it. citeturn41search16turn34view4turn32search1
- **Onboarding and help:** demonstrate actions with an animated hand/model before relying on text; use child-familiar verbs such as “tap,” “swipe,” and “drag.” citeturn26view0turn28view3
- **Mastery loop:** correctness → brief why → next best step → optional hint ladder → retry → skill-state update. Use more directive feedback for novices, fading toward lighter prompts as mastery grows. citeturn31view0turn30view1turn30view3turn35search3
- **Progress UI:** show a child’s own skill map or small set of current competencies; keep the learner model visible and navigable; avoid public ranking as a default. citeturn39view0turn38search7
- **AI safety UX:** always disclose that the tutor is AI, keep language literal and task-focused, and provide prominent “Ask an adult” and “Report this answer” tools. citeturn16view1turn34view3turn41search9

## Open questions and limitations

The strongest limitation in the research base is that there is much more evidence for **accessibility floors, cognitive-load principles, feedback design, and child interaction mechanics** than there is for one exact “best” font family, one exact pixel size, or one universally optimal child colour palette on contemporary AI tutor interfaces. The most defensible conclusion is therefore: start from accessibility standards, default larger and simpler for younger children, and make typography, motion, and contrast adjustable. citeturn22search28turn11view0turn11view1turn33view2

A second limitation is that some of the best work on learner dashboards, peer models, and adaptive feedback comes from older students rather than primary-age children. I have therefore been more confident in recommending **self-referenced mastery displays and strong error recovery** than in recommending any kind of social comparison or sophisticated metacognitive dashboard for younger learners. citeturn39view0turn38search7turn30view0

The practical takeaway is that, for your use case, the highest-confidence path is an accessibility-first, low-noise, skill-structured tutor whose interface behaves predictably, reveals mastery clearly, and teaches through short corrective loops rather than through spectacle. citeturn32search1turn33view3turn31view0
