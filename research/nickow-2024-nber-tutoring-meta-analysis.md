# The Promise of Tutoring for PreK-12 Learning: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of the Experimental Evidence

## Citation
Nickow, A., Oreopoulos, P., & Quan, V. (2024). The promise of tutoring for PreK-12 learning: A systematic review and meta-analysis of the experimental evidence. *American Educational Research Journal*, *61*(1), 74-107. https://doi.org/10.3102/00028312231208687

## Summary
This landmark meta-analysis represents the first comprehensive systematic review of experimental evidence on PreK-12 tutoring programs. Nickow, Oreopoulos, and Quan analysed randomised controlled trials conducted over several decades (1985-2020), covering one-on-one and small-group instructional programming delivered by teachers, paraprofessionals, volunteers, or parents. The authors developed a conceptual framework to understand the sources of variation in tutoring program outcomes across multiple design characteristics.

The results are striking: tutoring programs produce consistently substantial positive impacts on student learning, with a pooled effect size of 0.288 standard deviations. This makes tutoring one of the largest-impact educational interventions studied to date. The effect is robust across different program designs, though certain characteristics systematically predict larger impacts.

The study also reveals important nuance in moderator analyses. Programs using credentialled staff (teachers or paraprofessionals) as tutors, targeting earlier grades, delivering sessions at least three days per week, and operating during school hours all show the strongest results. Roughly 80% of the included studies evaluated literacy-focused interventions, though math tutoring also shows significant effects, particularly in later grades.

## Key Findings
- Overall pooled effect size: **ES = 0.288 SD** (SE = 0.029, p < 0.001) across 96 studies (89 studies yielding 732 effect estimates in one analysis count)
- Tutoring by teachers and paraprofessionals produces the largest effect sizes; volunteer- and parent-led models still show positive effects but are smaller
- Earlier grades show stronger effects: **0.41 SD for PreK and kindergarten** vs **0.13 SD for grades 6-11**
- Programs meeting at least 3 days per week during school hours produce larger impacts than lower-dosage or after-school programs
- Reading and math interventions show similar overall effects, but reading tutoring yields higher effect sizes in earlier grades while math tutoring yields higher effect sizes in later grades
- Nearly 80% of studies in the sample evaluated tutoring with a literacy component
- One-to-one tutoring formats produce stronger effects than small-group formats
- In-school tutoring yields larger impacts than after-school tutoring

## Methodology
- **Design**: Systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials (RCTs)
- **Sample**: 96 field experiments of PreK-12 tutoring programs (1985-2020), yielding 732 effect estimates
- **Inclusion criteria**: Experimental studies (RCTs) of tutoring interventions for PreK-12 students
- **Tutoring definition**: One-on-one or small-group instructional programming by teachers, paraprofessionals, volunteers, or parents
- **Analysis**: Random-effects meta-analysis with moderator analyses examining tutor type, grade level, dosage/frequency, in-school vs after-school delivery, subject area (reading vs math), and tutoring format (1:1 vs small group)
- **Published in**: *American Educational Research Journal* (AERJ), the flagship journal of the American Educational Research Association
- **Earlier version**: Originally circulated as NBER Working Paper No. 27476 (July 2020) with a reported pooled ES of 0.37 SD; the published 2024 AERJ version reports 0.288 SD after expanded analysis and peer review

## Relevance to Upwise
This meta-analysis provides foundational evidence for the core thesis behind Upwise: that personalised, one-on-one or small-group tutoring is among the most effective educational interventions available for PreK-12 students. Several findings directly inform product design:

1. **AI as a scalable tutor**: The 0.288 SD average effect establishes the benchmark Upwise should aim to match or approach. If AI-driven adaptive tutoring can approximate even a fraction of this effect at scale, the impact on Australian students could be substantial.

2. **Early grades and mastery learning**: The finding that younger students (PreK-K at 0.41 SD) benefit most from tutoring supports Upwise's focus on building strong foundations in early years through mastery-based progression aligned with ACARA curriculum.

3. **High-dosage design**: Programs with 3+ sessions per week show the strongest results, supporting Upwise's approach of encouraging regular daily practice through gamification and parent-guided engagement.

4. **Reading and math parity**: The finding that both literacy and numeracy tutoring are effective, with math tutoring particularly impactful in later grades, validates Upwise's multi-subject approach across the primary and secondary years.

5. **During-school vs after-school**: While Upwise operates primarily in the home/after-school context, the finding that in-school tutoring is more effective highlights the importance of making the at-home experience as engaging and structured as possible to overcome the motivational gap.
