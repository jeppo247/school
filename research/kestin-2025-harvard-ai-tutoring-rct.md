# AI Tutoring Outperforms In-Class Active Learning: An RCT Introducing a Novel Research-Based Design in an Authentic Educational Setting

## Citation
Kestin, G., Miller, K., Klales, A., Milbourne, T., & Ponti, G. (2025). AI tutoring outperforms in-class active learning: An RCT introducing a novel research-based design in an authentic educational setting. *Scientific Reports*, *15*(1), 17458. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-025-97652-6

## Summary
This randomized controlled trial, conducted at Harvard University in Fall 2023, compared student learning outcomes between a custom AI-powered tutor and traditional in-class active learning in an introductory physics course (Physical Sciences 2, N=194). The AI tutor was built on GPT-4 and designed with extensive prompt engineering to follow the same research-based pedagogical best practices used in the classroom: facilitating active learning, managing cognitive load, promoting a growth mindset, scaffolding content, ensuring accuracy through expert-crafted step-by-step solutions, providing timely personalised feedback, and enabling self-pacing.

The study used a crossover design where each student experienced both conditions across two consecutive weeks (surface tension and fluid flow topics). Students completed pre-tests and post-tests for each lesson. The results were striking: median learning gains in the AI-tutored group were over double those in the active learning group, a highly significant result (p < 10^-8). Students also spent less time learning with the AI tutor (median 49 minutes vs. 60 minutes in class) and reported significantly higher engagement and motivation.

The researchers emphasise that unguided use of AI chatbots does not produce these results. The key was careful pedagogical design: structured scaffolding, expert-written step-by-step solutions to prevent hallucinations, sequential problem guidance, and system prompts enforcing active learning principles. They recommend AI tutors as a complement to, not replacement for, in-person teaching, particularly for introducing material before class time.

## Key Findings
- **Learning gains doubled**: Median post-test score of 4.5 (AI group) vs. 3.5 (active learning group), against a baseline pre-test median of 2.75 (z = -5.6, p < 10^-8)
- **Large effect size**: Linear regression effect size of d = 0.63 (underestimate due to ceiling effect); quantile regression estimate of d = 0.73 to 1.3 standard deviations
- **Less time required**: Median time on task for AI group was 49 minutes vs. 60 minutes for in-class group; 70% of AI students finished in under 60 minutes
- **Higher engagement**: AI group mean 4.1/5 vs. active learning 3.6/5 (t(311) = -4.5, p < 0.0001)
- **Higher motivation**: AI group mean 3.4/5 vs. active learning 3.1/5 (t(311) = -3.4, p < 0.001)
- **No difference in enjoyment or growth mindset** between the two groups
- **Self-pacing validated**: Students who found class "too fast" all spent more than median time on AI; those who found it "too slow" all spent less
- **Accuracy perception**: 83% of students reported AI tutor explanations were as good as or better than human instructors
- **No correlation** between time on task and post-test scores in AI group, suggesting self-pacing naturally adjusts
- **Results held across ability levels**: Significant for students with FCI scores both below and above 40% (p < 0.001 for both subgroups)
- **Citation impact**: 44 citations within weeks of publication; 99.9th percentile citation impact (FWCI = 65.42)

## Methodology
- **Design**: Randomised controlled trial with crossover design (each student experienced both conditions)
- **Sample**: N = 194 eligible students (from 233 enrolled) in Harvard's Physical Sciences 2 (introductory physics for life sciences), the university's largest physics class
- **Population**: College students broadly representative of a range of institutions, with FCI pretest scores spanning the 30-50% range typical across universities
- **Conditions**: (1) AI-powered tutor at home, (2) In-class active learning with instructor and course staff
- **Topics**: Surface tension (Week 1) and fluid flow (Week 2), with conditions reversed between groups
- **Measures**: Pre-test and post-test content mastery assessments (Bloom's Taxonomy levels: understanding, applying, analysing); 4-item perception survey (engagement, enjoyment, motivation, growth mindset) on 5-point Likert scale
- **AI Technology**: Custom tutor built on GPT-4, with detailed system prompts enforcing pedagogical best practices and expert-crafted step-by-step solutions to prevent hallucinations; sequential scaffolding of multi-part problems
- **Controls in regression model**: Pre-test score, midterm exam score, Force Concept Inventory (FCI), prior ChatGPT experience, class topic, test version, time on task; clustered at student level
- **Statistical tests**: Mann-Whitney rank-sum test, linear regression, quantile regression (to address ceiling effects), dependent t-tests for perception measures

## Relevance to Upwise
This study provides the strongest RCT evidence to date that AI tutoring, when designed with sound pedagogical principles, can dramatically outperform even high-quality active learning instruction. Several findings directly support Upwise's product design:

- **Mastery learning works with AI**: The AI tutor's scaffolded, sequential approach (ensuring understanding before moving on) mirrors mastery-based learning. Students who needed more time took it; those who already understood moved faster. This validates Upwise's adaptive mastery model.
- **Personalised feedback is the mechanism**: The researchers attribute the gains primarily to personalised, on-demand feedback and self-pacing, which are impossible in a one-teacher-many-students classroom. This is exactly what Upwise delivers.
- **Pedagogical design is non-negotiable**: Unstructured AI chatbot use produces poor results (the paper cites prior studies showing declines). The carefully designed system prompts, step-by-step expert solutions, and structured scaffolding are what made the difference. Upwise's ACARA-aligned, curriculum-structured approach follows this principle.
- **Self-pacing benefits all ability levels**: Lower-performing and higher-performing students both benefited, validating Upwise's approach of meeting each child where they are.
- **Engagement without novelty dependency**: Students reported higher engagement and motivation, and the crossover design (every student tries both) controls for novelty effects.
- **Complement, not replacement**: The researchers recommend AI tutoring for introducing material before class, with class time reserved for higher-order skills. This aligns with Upwise's positioning as a home learning support tool alongside school education.
