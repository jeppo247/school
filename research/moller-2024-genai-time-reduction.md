# Revolutionising Distance Learning: A Comparative Study of Learning Progress with AI-Driven Tutoring

## Citation

Moller, M., Nirmal, G., Fabietti, D., Stierstorfer, Q., Zakhvatkin, M., Sommerfeld, H., & Schutt, S. (2024). Revolutionising distance learning: A comparative study of learning progress with AI-driven tutoring. arXiv preprint arXiv:2403.14642. https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.14642

## Summary

This study provides early large-scale evidence that generative AI tutoring can meaningfully accelerate university-level learning. Researchers at IU International University of Applied Sciences examined whether Syntea, an AI-powered teaching assistant with an exam training feature, affected the speed at which distance learning students progressed through their courses. Using a quasi-experimental design comparing Syntea users to non-users across more than 40 courses and 24 months of baseline data, the study found that students using the AI tutor reduced their effective study time by approximately 27% by the third month after the tool's release.

The analysis was carefully constructed to account for selection bias and seasonal effects. While treatment group students (those who used Syntea at least once) were already faster learners at baseline, the critical finding was that the gap between the two groups widened significantly after Syntea's release, with the relative difference in study progression growing from a baseline of 24.1% to 69.9% by the third month post-release. This widening gap -- not the absolute difference -- constitutes the core evidence of Syntea's effect.

Importantly, this acceleration came with minimal quality trade-offs. Pass rates remained virtually unchanged (98% for control, 97% for treatment post-release), and while a small grade decrease of 3.6% emerged by month three, the authors characterised this as negligible compared to the magnitude of the acceleration effect. The study's limitations include the lack of randomised assignment and the absence of dose-response analysis.

## Key Findings

- **Primary effect**: ~27% reduction in study time by the third month post-release
- **Study progression increase**: relative difference grew from 24.1% (baseline) to 69.9% (month 3), a widening of +45.8 percentage points (t = 5.92, p < 0.001)
- **Month-by-month progression**: month 0 = +3.8 pp (p = 0.584, NS); month 1 = +14.9 pp (p = 0.031); month 2 = +45.8 pp (p < 0.001)
- **Practical translation**: a course taking a control student 100 days would take a Syntea user ~81 days at baseline, dropping to 59 days by month 3
- **Pass rates preserved**: 98% control vs 97% treatment post-release; no significant difference (month 2: t = -1.68, p = 0.093)
- **Small grade decrease**: treatment group grades dropped from 80.72 to 78.31 (out of 100) by month 2; pre-post change = -3.4 pp (t = -4.23, p < 0.001), though authors note effect size is small relative to acceleration
- **Scale**: 40+ courses spanning business, technical, and other subject areas
- **Observation counts**: 1,110,788 control observations (pre-release); 13,178 treatment observations (pre-release); ~960-1,010 treatment observations per post-release month

## Methodology

- **Design**: quasi-experimental, pre-post comparison with treatment (Syntea users) and control (non-users) groups
- **Setting**: IU International University of Applied Sciences, distance learning programme
- **Intervention**: Syntea AI-powered teaching assistant with exam training feature, released end of September 2023
- **Time period**: October 2021 to December 2023 (24 months pre-release baseline, 3 months post-release)
- **Treatment definition**: students who used the Syntea exam trainer feature at least once
- **Primary outcome**: study progression (average exams passed per student per month), inverted to study duration (months per exam)
- **Statistical approach**: two-tiered -- (1) explorative 60-day sliding window analysis, (2) month-level aggregated t-tests comparing pre-post relative differences
- **Baseline correction**: month-by-month control group averages used to calculate relative differences, controlling for seasonality
- **Sensitivity check**: excluded students joining after January 2023 to rule out population composition effects; results unchanged
- **Limitations acknowledged**: selection bias in absolute comparisons (treatment group faster at baseline); no randomisation; no dose-response analysis; small grade decrease at month 3; confidence intervals not reported

## Relevance to Upwise

This study has several direct implications for Upwise's product strategy:

1. **AI tutoring accelerates learning, not just improves it**: the 27% time reduction finding reframes the value proposition. For time-pressed Australian families, Upwise can position adaptive AI tutoring as achieving the same learning outcomes in meaningfully less time -- turning a 30-minute practice session into the equivalent of 40 minutes of unaided study.

2. **Effect grows with sustained use**: the progression from non-significant at month 0 to highly significant at month 2 suggests that AI tutoring benefits compound as students learn to use the tool effectively. This supports Upwise's subscription model and argues for onboarding flows that encourage consistent early engagement.

3. **Quality is preserved**: the near-identical pass rates (98% vs 97%) counter the concern that AI-assisted learning might reduce depth. Upwise can cite this as evidence that AI-adaptive practice maintains rigour while accelerating mastery.

4. **Works across diverse subjects**: the 40+ course coverage spanning business to technical fields suggests the approach generalises well, supporting Upwise's plan to expand beyond mathematics into other ACARA curriculum areas.

5. **Exam practice is the key feature**: Syntea's primary mechanism was exam-relevant practice with immediate feedback -- precisely the adaptive quiz-and-feedback loop that Upwise's mastery system implements. This validates the product's core pedagogical mechanism.
