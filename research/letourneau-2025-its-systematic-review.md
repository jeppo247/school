# A Systematic Review of AI-Driven Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) in K-12 Education

## Citation
Letourneau, A., Deslandes Martineau, M., Charland, P., Karran, J. A., Boasen, J., & Leger, P.-M. (2025). A systematic review of AI-driven intelligent tutoring systems (ITS) in K-12 education. *npj Science of Learning*, *10*(1), 29. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41539-025-00320-7

## Summary
This systematic review, conducted by researchers at Universite du Quebec a Montreal (UQAM) and HEC Montreal, examined 28 studies encompassing 4,597 students to assess the effects of AI-driven intelligent tutoring systems on K-12 students' learning and performance. The review followed PRISMA guidelines and searched the ERIC and Scopus databases. Studies spanned eight countries (54% from the USA, 27% from Asia, 15% from Europe), covered grade levels from elementary through high school, and focused predominantly on STEM subjects (82%), particularly mathematics, with the remainder in language arts.

The findings are broadly positive: when comparing ITS to traditional teacher-led instruction, seven out of eight studies (87.5%) reported significant positive effects with medium to large effect sizes. However, the picture becomes more nuanced with different comparison conditions. When ITS was compared to non-intelligent (non-adaptive) tutoring systems, only one of four studies found a significant advantage for the intelligent system. When different ITS implementations were compared against each other, the research identified specific features that optimise effectiveness rather than blanket superiority. The review highlights that personalisation, adaptive feedback, mastery-based progression, and self-regulation support are the core components driving ITS effectiveness.

A critical finding is that many studies used very short intervention durations (half lasted less than one week), raising concerns about novelty effects inflating results. Only one study (Holstein et al.) acknowledged this potential bias. The review calls for longer interventions, larger and more diverse samples (particularly including younger students and non-STEM subjects), and attention to the ethical implications of AI in education, which none of the 28 studies addressed.

## Key Findings

### Overall effectiveness
- **28 studies** analysed, **N = 4,597 students** total
- **ITS vs. Teacher**: 7 of 8 studies (87.5%) showed ITS significantly outperformed traditional teaching, with medium to large effect sizes
- **ITS vs. Non-intelligent TS**: Only 1 of 4 studies showed significant advantage for ITS; 3 found no significant difference
- **ITS vs. Modified ITS**: 11 studies compared different ITS configurations; only 3 provided effect sizes (mostly small)
- **ITS vs. No control**: 4 studies showed general improvements but lacked comparison groups

### Specific effect sizes reported across studies
- Cui et al.: Learning gains 4.19x greater with ITS vs. teacher; Hedges' g = 0.68 (medium)
- Dolenc et al.: ITS vs. national test benchmarks; d = 0.99 and d = 1.30 (large)
- Wijekumar et al.: Planning skills d = 0.77 (medium) for ITS vs. teacher
- Nehring et al.: d = 0.87 for ALEKS ITS vs. traditional instruction over 30 weeks
- Roscoe et al.: Essay scores improved d = 0.71 with Writing Pal ITS over 6 months
- Pane et al. (referenced): +0.20 SD for Cognitive Tutor in high schools after sustained implementation (equivalent to an extra year of instruction)
- VanLehn (referenced): Step-based ITS achieve effect sizes of 0.75-0.80, comparable to human tutoring at 0.79

### Features driving effectiveness
- **Personalisation and adaptivity** are core components (Yixue, UZWEBMAT)
- **Immediate, real-time feedback** outperforms delayed feedback (Rimac, TECH8)
- **Mastery-based progression** helps reduce learning gaps
- **Self-regulation features** (skill diaries, reflection prompts) improve outcomes
- **Teacher integration** (blended models) enhances results beyond ITS alone
- **Deep personalisation** with high learner engagement produced large effect sizes (d = 0.92)
- **Middle school students** showed more pronounced gains than high school students
- **Lower-performing students** exhibited greater benefits due to tailored scaffolding

### Study characteristics
- **54% of studies** involved high school students; 32% middle school; 14% elementary
- **82% focused on STEM** (primarily mathematics and physics); 18% on language arts
- **62% of authors** from educational science backgrounds (more reliable for learning outcomes assessment)
- **0% of studies** addressed AI ethics considerations
- Most studies used **quasi-experimental designs** with pre- and post-tests
- **Half of interventions lasted less than one week**, raising novelty effect concerns

## Methodology
- **Design**: Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines
- **Databases**: ERIC USDE (Education Resources Information Center) and Scopus
- **Inclusion criteria**: Studies evaluating effects of AI-driven ITS on K-12 students' learning and performance using experimental or quasi-experimental designs
- **Studies analysed**: 28 studies from 26 publications (2 articles each contained 2 studies)
- **Total participants**: N = 4,597 students
- **Countries represented**: 8 countries (USA 54%, Taiwan, China, Thailand, Korea, Turkey, Saudi Arabia, and European nations including Slovenia, Hungary, Spain, Netherlands)
- **Grade levels**: Elementary (14%), middle school (32%), high school (54%)
- **Subjects**: STEM 82% (primarily mathematics, also physics, computer science), language arts 18%
- **Control group categories**: ITS vs. Teacher (8 studies), ITS vs. Non-intelligent TS (5 studies), ITS vs. Modified ITS (11 studies), ITS vs. No control (4 studies)
- **ITS platforms studied**: Cognitive Tutor, Yixue Squirrel AI, ALEKS PPL, Writing Pal, iSTART, TECH8, UZWEBMAT, Rimac, Lynnette/Lumilo, Duolingo, iTutor, Zenbo, MathTutor, GATT/MIATS, WeWrite, ARGeoITS, BOXFiSH, among others
- **Intervention durations**: Ranged from a single class period to 30 weeks; median duration was short (half lasted less than one week)

## Relevance to Upwise
This comprehensive review of the K-12 ITS landscape directly validates Upwise's core product decisions and highlights opportunities:

- **ITS effectiveness is proven for K-12, especially vs. traditional teaching**: The 87.5% positive finding rate when compared to teacher-led instruction confirms that well-designed AI tutoring meaningfully improves learning outcomes for school-age children. This is the market Upwise serves.
- **Mathematics is the most validated subject**: The overwhelming focus on maths aligns with Upwise's ACARA numeracy curriculum alignment. The evidence base is strongest exactly where Upwise is building.
- **Personalisation and adaptivity are the differentiators**: The review identifies these as the core components driving effectiveness, not the technology itself. When ITS was compared to non-intelligent systems, the advantage was smaller. This means Upwise's adaptive, mastery-based approach is the right strategic bet, not just having AI for AI's sake.
- **Mastery-based progression is specifically called out**: The review highlights that mastery-based learning support "provides a foundation upon which students can build progress toward more complex topics only once they have demonstrated proficiency in prerequisite concepts, thereby helping to reduce learning gaps." This is Upwise's pedagogical model.
- **Middle school students benefit most**: Students in the middle school range showed more pronounced gains than high school. Upwise's target demographic (primary and early secondary) aligns with where ITS has the greatest impact.
- **Lower-performing students gain the most**: The tailored scaffolding ITS provides disproportionately benefits students who are behind, supporting Upwise's equity-oriented positioning.
- **Short interventions may inflate results**: The review warns that novelty effects from brief studies could overstate benefits. Upwise's design for sustained, daily practice (not one-off sessions) addresses this concern and may yield more durable gains.
- **Teacher/parent integration matters**: Studies combining ITS with human oversight (teacher dashboards, blended models) showed enhanced results. Upwise's parent dashboard and guided learning model reflect this finding.
- **Ethics gap is a market opportunity**: None of the 28 studies addressed AI ethics. Upwise can differentiate by building transparent, responsible AI practices into the product from day one, particularly important for the Australian market where parents are privacy-conscious.
- **Self-regulation and gamification enhance engagement**: Features like goal-setting, skill tracking, and deep personalisation boosted both engagement and outcomes. Upwise's themed, gamified approach directly implements these evidence-based features.
